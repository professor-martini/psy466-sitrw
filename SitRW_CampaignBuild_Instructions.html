Campaign Build & Process Documentation

This assignment asks you to build your campaign deliberately, document your thinking as you go, and show — not just describe — how your work evolved from first attempt to finished product.

What this assignment actually is

20 points

This assignment is not about producing a polished final product. It is about making your decision-making visible.

Anyone can produce a finished campaign. What this assignment asks you to show is that you thought carefully about every component you built — that you made deliberate choices grounded in your research and your audience, considered alternatives, identified what wasn't working, and revised intentionally.

That is how science communicators, public health educators, and media designers actually work. The first version is never the final version. Every revision is a decision. This assignment asks you to show your work.

The core question Dr. Martini is asking Is this student actually thinking about what they are making and why? A rough campaign with deeply thoughtful process documentation will score higher than a polished campaign with a superficial log.

What to submit

Your submission has three parts, all due at the same time.

Part A — Your campaign visuals or video links

You must submit the actual creative work — not just a description of it. This is how Dr. Martini verifies that the decisions you describe in your documentation actually happened.

Instagram carousel

Export each version as a .png or .pdf from Canva. Drag and drop directly into your Word document alongside your reflection for each version.

TikTok / Reels

Post each version to your mock account and paste the direct link into your Word document next to your reflection for that version.

Show, don't just describe If your reflection says "I changed the color of Slide 2," Dr. Martini needs to see Slide 2 before and after. The visual evidence is what makes your documentation verifiable. Reflections without accompanying visuals or links cannot receive full credit.

Part B — Your Process Documentation (Word document)

One Word document containing: your Deep Reflection, your Version Log, your Completion Confirmations, and your AI Use Log. The Word document template is provided in Canvas.

Part C — AI Use Log (Excel)

A separate Excel file documenting every AI interaction on this assignment. The template is provided in Canvas. If you did not use AI, a single row stating "No AI tools were used" is sufficient.

Instagram: infographic ≠ PowerPoint An Instagram carousel is a designed visual product — it uses layout, color, typography, and graphics. A PowerPoint deck saved as images is not an infographic. If you have not already, use Canva (free) to build your carousel. This distinction matters for your grade.
★

The deep reflection

12 of 20 points

Choose one component from your campaign — one slide (Instagram) or one video (TikTok) — and complete a deep reflection on it. Choose the component where you made the most interesting or difficult decisions. Choosing is itself a thinking task: it requires you to evaluate your own work and identify where the hardest creative and intellectual work happened.

Initial version of this component → Revised version of this component

Your deep reflection documents both versions. You embed or link the initial version, answer the questions below about it, then embed or link the revised version and document what changed and why.

This works the same whether or not you used AI If you used AI on this component: your reflection describes what AI produced and what you changed. If you did not use AI: your reflection describes what your first attempt looked like and what you changed. The questions are identical either way. Neither path is privileged.

Part 1 — Before your revision

Embed or link your initial version here, then answer the following.

Q1 What claim is this component making, and which specific finding from which specific article supports it?

Name the article (author + year), describe the specific finding, and explain in your own words how your component communicates that finding to a 10th grader. If your component makes more than one claim, address each one separately.

Q2 What did you try first, and why didn't it work? ★ Hard to fake

Describe your initial approach to this component — what you made before you revised it. What specifically wasn't working? Was the research unclear? The language wrong for the audience? The visual not communicating what you intended? Be honest and specific. If you used AI to generate an initial version, describe what it produced and what the problem was with it.

Level of specificity expected
"My first version wasn't very good so I changed it." "My first version used the phrase 'asymptomatic transmission' directly from the article. When I read it back imagining a 16-year-old, I realized that phrase means nothing to most people. The research finding was there but it wasn't landing."
Q3 Is anything in this version potentially inaccurate or overstated?

Check every claim in your initial version against your articles. Is there anything a viewer might walk away believing that goes beyond what your research actually supports? Flag it here even if you plan to fix it in the revision.

Part 2 — Your revision decisions

Q4 What specific decision did you make, what alternatives did you consider and reject, and why? ★ Hard to fake

This is the most important question in the assignment. Describe the specific creative or editorial decision you made when revising this component. Then — and this is critical — describe at least one alternative you considered and explain why you rejected it in favor of the approach you chose.

This question cannot be answered well without having been present for the decision. It requires you to have weighed options and chosen. Generic rationales ("I made it clearer") will not receive credit.

What "alternatives considered" looks like
"I decided to use simpler language to make it more accessible." "I considered replacing 'asymptomatic' with a definition in parentheses, but that made the slide too text-heavy. I also considered cutting the technical term entirely, but that felt like I was dumbing it down rather than explaining it. I settled on leading with the plain-language version ('no symptoms doesn't mean no infection') and putting the clinical term in smaller text below, so students who want the word have it."
Q5 Is your content appropriate and inclusive for 10th–11th graders?

Look at your revised version specifically. Is the language accessible without being condescending? Is the tone non-shaming and non-judgmental? Does anything risk excluding, alienating, or embarrassing a student based on gender, orientation, or relationship status? Be honest.

Part 3 — After your revision

Embed or link your revised version here, then answer the following.

Q6 What are you still not satisfied with? ★ Hard to fake

Name something about the revised version that you are still not happy with — a decision you are uncertain about, a tradeoff you made that still bothers you, or something you would change if you had more time. This is not penalized. It is the clearest evidence of genuine critical engagement with your own work. AI almost never produces genuine dissatisfaction with its own output. You should.

The version log

4 points

The version log documents how your full campaign changed from your initial attempt to your final version. This is distinct from the deep reflection, which focuses on one component in depth. The version log looks at the campaign as a whole.

Initial campaign (all components) → Final campaign (all components)

Submit your initial full campaign (all slides or all videos) and your final full campaign, then complete the version log questions in the Word document template.

Version log questions

VL1What changed across the campaign from initial to final?

Go through each component. Describe specifically what changed. "I improved it" is not sufficient. "I moved the source attribution from inside the body text to a smaller line at the bottom because it was visually competing with the main claim" is the level of specificity this question requires.

VL2Did any changes in one component affect others?

Sometimes revising one component reveals a problem in another, or forces a change to maintain consistency. Did that happen? Describe it.

VL3How does your final campaign fulfill the campaign requirements?

Walk through each requirement and confirm it is met — or describe what still needs to be addressed before the final submission.

  • All slides/videos are present and complete
  • Content is grounded in your empirical articles with specific findings attributed to specific sources
  • Language is appropriate for 10th–11th graders
  • Messaging is inclusive and non-shaming
  • Sources are attributed in the campaign
5

Completion confirmations (remaining components)

2 points

For each component not chosen for your deep reflection, answer one question. This keeps accountability across the whole campaign without requiring a full deep reflection on every component.

CCWhat was the hardest decision you made on this component, and why?

One to three sentences per component. Name the specific decision, explain what made it hard, and describe what you chose. This is not graded for depth — it is graded for honesty and specificity. "I had trouble deciding what color to use" will not receive credit. "I couldn't decide whether to use the 62% statistic from Forhan et al. or the 38% from Satterwhite et al. because they measure different things and I didn't want to mislead — I chose the Forhan number because it's more directly comparable to my audience" will.

AI use policy and log

2 points

This assignment is designed to work equally well whether you use AI or not. The documentation requirements are identical for both paths. Neither approach is penalized.

✓ Permitted uses
  • → Generating caption ideas
  • → Suggesting color palettes or layouts
  • → Drafting slide text or scripts
  • → Refining language for a teen audience
  • → Brainstorming visual concepts
✗ Not permitted
  • → Generating research findings
  • → Summarizing your empirical articles
  • → Interpreting what studies mean
  • → Writing your process documentation
  • → Answering the reflection questions

For every AI interaction, complete one row in the AI Use Log (Excel template provided). The most important column is "What I Did With It" — explain whether you kept, modified, or rejected the AI output, and why. Uncritical acceptance of AI output will receive minimal credit.

If you did not use AI at any point on this assignment, write "No AI tools were used" in the first row of the log. That is all that is required.

Choose the component where you made the most difficult or interesting decisions — the one where you had to think hardest about how to translate your research for your audience, where something wasn't working and you had to figure out why, or where you made a choice you can actually explain and defend.

Do not choose the component that came out best. Choose the one that taught you something.

If your versions are nearly identical, that is a signal that either (a) you did not revise meaningfully, which will affect your grade, or (b) you are not looking carefully enough at what changed. Even small changes — a word, a number, a layout decision — can be the subject of a strong reflection if you can explain why you made them.

The version log and deep reflection require real differences to document. If your initial and final campaigns are identical, there is nothing to reflect on.
