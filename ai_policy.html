The core rule

Guiding principle

AI may be used as a creative production tool, but not as a substitute for your own learning, reading, analysis, synthesis, or communication of course material.

Put more simply: AI can help you make it look good. It cannot do the thinking for you.

You are responsible for understanding the science, selecting your sources, reading and evaluating those sources, and deciding what to communicate to your audience. Those are the parts that actually teach you something — and they are the parts AI is not allowed to replace.

Where AI earns its place in this course is on the creative and production side of the Sex in the Real World project: generating images, suggesting visual layouts, refining how your already-written content looks and feels. Using it to find your articles, summarize your research, or write your project description is not permitted.

The test to apply in the moment If you removed AI's contribution, would you still have done the intellectual work of the assignment? If yes, you're probably fine. If no — if AI did the thinking, the reading, or the explaining — that's a problem.

Permitted

Go ahead — and disclose it

This is a legitimate creative/production use of AI. You may proceed — but you must disclose it in your final reflection. Note which tool you used, what you used it for, and what parts of the project you completed yourself. See Section 5 for exactly what that disclosure needs to include.

Permitted

Fine — with one caveat

Using AI to check grammar or spelling in writing you've already produced is permitted. Just make sure the tool isn't substantially rewriting your sentences. If you paste a paragraph in and get a different paragraph back, that crosses into prohibited territory. If you used it for the SitRW project, disclose it.

Permitted

A legitimate study tool

Generating practice questions or flashcards for your own studying is fine. Just never use AI to generate answers you then submit — that defeats the purpose of the assignment and cheats you out of learning the material.

Permitted — with caution

Fine for your own understanding, not for submitting

Using AI to help you understand a concept is fine — it's not much different from Googling something. But be careful: AI can produce confident-sounding but inaccurate information, especially on nuanced scientific topics. If something AI tells you conflicts with the textbook or lecture, trust the course material. Never submit AI-generated answers as your own work.

Not permitted

This is academic misconduct

Using AI to generate answers for reading quizzes or lecture video questions is not permitted. Beyond the policy violation, it also cheats you — these assignments build the foundation you need for the SitRW project. Students who bypass them consistently struggle with the project later. If you're struggling with the content, reach out to Dr. M. or use the tutoring resources in the syllabus.

Not permitted

This is the intellectual core of the project

You must personally choose your topic, find and read your empirical articles, understand what they found, identify the misconceptions your audience holds, and decide what science to communicate. AI may not do any of this work for you. Submitting AI-generated content as your own work in these areas is academic misconduct.

Not permitted

Your words, your thinking

AI may not write, draft, or substantially rewrite your discussion posts, reflections, or other written assignments. Submitting AI-generated text as your own work is academic misconduct regardless of how much you edited it afterward.

Not permitted

The script is the educational content

The script, captions, and slide text are the educational content of your project — they need to come from you and your sources. AI may help you refine the presentation style of content you've already written, but it may not generate the content itself. If AI wrote your script, the project doesn't represent your understanding of the science.

Permitted uses — and how AI can actually help you

Here's where AI can genuinely make your work better — specifically on the creative and production side of the Sex in the Real World project.

Final Social Media Campaign Submission

Generating images and visual elements

What this looks like in practice

Use tools like DALL-E, Adobe Firefly, Midjourney, or Canva AI to generate background images, illustrations, icons, or infographic templates. For example: generate a warm, non-clinical illustration of two people having a conversation as a background for your first TikTok slide. The image serves your message — you're not asking AI to create the message.

Final Social Media Campaign Submission

Suggesting layout and design structure

What this looks like in practice

Ask AI to suggest infographic layouts, color schemes, font pairings, or slide structures. For example: "I have five key facts about chlamydia transmission — what Instagram carousel layout would work well for a teen audience?" You decide which suggestions to use. You're still driving the content and the decisions.

Final Social Media Campaign Submission

Polishing captions and hooks

What this looks like in practice

After you've written your caption or hook, you can ask AI to suggest ways to make it more engaging for a teen audience — as long as the scientific content, the core message, and the source selection all came from you. Think of it like asking a friend to read your caption and suggest whether it sounds natural.

All stages

Grammar and spelling checks

What this looks like in practice

Basic grammar and spell checking in writing you've already produced is fine — in any assignment. The line is whether AI is correcting your errors or rewriting your sentences. If your paragraph comes back substantially different, that's rewriting, not editing.

Studying

Generating practice questions for studying

What this looks like in practice

Ask AI to generate practice questions based on a chapter summary or concept you're reviewing. This is a legitimate study tool. Never use it to generate answers you then submit — that defeats the whole purpose.

RememberAny AI use on the SitRW project — even permitted use — requires disclosure in your final reflection. See Section 5.

Disclosure — what's required and how to do it

If you use AI at any stage of the Sex in the Real World project — even for something fully permitted — you must disclose that use in your final reflection. This applies even to image generation or layout suggestions.

ImportantDisclosing permitted AI use will never get you in trouble. Students who disclose permitted use are fine. Students who don't disclose any use — permitted or not — are in violation of the policy.

Your disclosure needs to cover four things:

  • Which AI tool you used (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Canva AI, Adobe Firefly, DALL-E, Midjourney)
  • How you used it (e.g., image generation, layout suggestions, caption refinement, grammar check)
  • What parts of the project were AI-assisted
  • What parts of the project you completed yourself
Sample disclosure I used Canva AI to generate the background image for my first Instagram slide and to suggest two possible carousel layout options — I chose one and modified it. I used ChatGPT to check the grammar in my captions after I had written them; I accepted a few minor corrections but no substantive rewrites. I did not use AI to select my topic, find or read my empirical articles, identify the misconceptions I targeted, or write the scientific content of the project. All source selection, reading, synthesis, and educational content decisions were made by me.
Grammarly noteBasic spell and grammar check features are fine on personal devices. Grammarly's "rewrite sentence," "improve tone," and similar generative features cross into AI-generated content and require disclosure if used. The public version of Grammarly is banned on state-owned devices entirely.

Consequences

Misuse of AI in this course is treated as academic misconduct under the NS Student Code of Conduct and the LASB Academic Conduct Policy. Consequences scale with severity and frequency.

SituationLikely outcome
First violation, minor (e.g., undisclosed permitted use)Warning and required resubmission
First violation, significant (e.g., AI-written Project Description)Zero on the assignment; academic misconduct report filed
Repeated violations or severe misconductF in the course, academic probation, suspension, or expulsion
The most important thing to know"I didn't know" is not a defense. A misunderstanding about the AI policy does not excuse submitting work that violates the expectations of the course. If you're ever uncertain about a specific use, email Dr. M. before you do it — not after. Contact Dr. M. through the Canvas Inbox or at pete.martini@nevadastate.edu.
