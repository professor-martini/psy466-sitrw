1
The core rule
Guiding Principle
AI may be used as a creative production tool, but not as a substitute for your own learning, reading, analysis, synthesis, or communication of course material.

Put even more simply: AI can help you make it look good. It cannot do the thinking for you.

In this course you are responsible for understanding the science, selecting your sources, reading and evaluating those sources, and deciding what to communicate to your audience. Those are the parts that actually teach you something — and they are the parts AI is not allowed to replace.

Where AI earns its place in this course is on the creative and production side of the Sex in the Real World project: generating images, suggesting visual layouts, refining how your already-written content looks and feels. That's a legitimate use of the tool. Using it to find your articles, summarize your research, or write your project description is not.

The test to apply in the moment: If you removed the AI's contribution, would you still have done the intellectual work of the assignment? If yes, you're probably fine. If no — if AI did the thinking, the reading, or the explaining — that's a problem.

Permitted uses — and how AI can actually help you

Here's where AI can genuinely make your work better — specifically on the creative and production side of the Sex in the Real World project.

Final Social Media Campaign Submission
Generating images and visual elements
What this looks like in practice
Use tools like DALL-E, Adobe Firefly, Midjourney, or Canva AI to generate background images, illustrations, icons, or infographic templates. For example: "generate a warm, non-clinical illustration of two people having a conversation" as a background for your first TikTok slide. The image serves your message — you're not asking AI to create the message.
Final Social Media Campaign Submission
Suggesting layout and design structure
What this looks like in practice
Ask AI to suggest infographic layouts, color schemes, font pairings, or slide structures. For example: "I have five key facts about chlamydia transmission — what Instagram carousel layout would work well for a teen audience?" You decide which suggestions to use. You're still driving the content and the decisions.
Final Social Media Campaign Submission
Polishing captions and hooks
What this looks like in practice
After you've written your caption or hook, you can ask AI to suggest ways to make it more engaging for a teen audience — as long as the scientific content, the core message, and the source selection all came from you. Think of it like asking a friend to read your caption and suggest whether it sounds natural. The ideas are still yours.
All stages
Grammar and spelling checks
What this looks like in practice
Basic grammar and spell checking in writing you've already produced is fine — in any assignment. The line is whether AI is correcting your errors or rewriting your sentences. If your paragraph comes back substantially different, that's rewriting, not editing.
Studying
Generating practice questions for studying
What this looks like in practice
Ask AI to generate practice questions based on a chapter summary or concept you're reviewing. This is a legitimate study tool. Just never use it to generate answers you then submit — that defeats the whole purpose.
Remember: Any AI use on the SitRW project — even permitted use — requires disclosure in your final reflection. See Section 5.
4
Prohibited uses

These are the uses that cross the line — either because they replace your thinking, or because they undermine the learning the assignment is designed to produce.

What you might be tempted to doWhy it's a problem
Use AI to answer reading quiz or lecture video questionsThese assignments build the foundation you need for the SitRW project. Bypassing them doesn't just violate policy — it leaves you without the knowledge you need to do the rest of the course well. You're cheating yourself more than anything.
Ask AI to suggest or select your SitRW topicTopic selection is the first genuine intellectual decision of the project. It requires you to think about what you care about, what the science supports, and what your audience needs. AI can't do that for you.
Use AI to find, screen, or summarize your empirical articlesAI frequently hallucinates sources — it invents citations that look real but don't exist. Beyond that, reading your articles yourself is the only way to actually understand the science you're supposed to be communicating.
Ask AI to write or rewrite your project descriptionThe project description is where you demonstrate that you understand your research, your audience, and your approach. If AI writes it, there's nothing for Dr. M. to give feedback on — and nothing for you to have learned.
Use AI to generate the script, slide text, or educational content of your final campaignThis is the whole project. The campaign is supposed to represent your understanding of the science. If AI generated it, it represents AI's output, not yours.
Use AI to "clean up" writing that ends up substantially rewrittenIf you paste a paragraph in and a different paragraph comes back, that's AI-generated content — regardless of whether you typed the first draft. The test is whether the ideas and sentences are still yours.
Submit AI-generated content without disclosureEven permitted AI use requires disclosure. Submitting any AI-assisted work without attribution is a violation of the policy.
A note on reading quizzes and lecture videos specifically: It might be tempting to think these are low-stakes enough to cut corners on. They're not — they're 70% of your grade and they're specifically designed to make sure you're keeping up with the material in a condensed 6-week course. More importantly, the knowledge you build through those assignments is what makes the SitRW project possible. Students who bypass them consistently struggle with the project later.
5
Disclosure — what's required and how to do it

If you use AI at any stage of the Sex in the Real World project — even for something fully permitted — you must disclose that use in your final reflection. This is not optional, and it applies even to image generation or layout suggestions.

Disclosing permitted AI use will never get you in trouble. The disclosure requirement exists so Dr. M. can understand what you produced and what AI assisted with. It is not a trap. Students who disclose permitted use are fine. Students who don't disclose any use — permitted or not — are in violation of the policy.

Your disclosure needs to cover four things:

  • Which AI tool you used (e.g., ChatGPT, Claude, Canva AI, Adobe Firefly, DALL-E, Midjourney)
  • How you used it (e.g., image generation, layout suggestions, caption refinement, grammar check)
  • What parts of the project were AI-assisted
  • What parts of the project you completed yourself

Here's what a complete, well-written disclosure looks like:

Sample disclosure
I used Canva AI to generate the background image for my first Instagram slide and to suggest two possible carousel layout options — I chose one and modified it. I used ChatGPT to check the grammar in my captions after I had written them; I accepted a few minor corrections but no substantive rewrites. I did not use AI to select my topic, find or read my empirical articles, identify the misconceptions I targeted, or write the scientific content of the project. All source selection, reading, synthesis, and educational content decisions were made by me.

Notice what this disclosure does well: it's specific about which tools were used and for what, it's clear about what AI contributed versus what the student contributed, and it demonstrates that the intellectual core of the project came from the student.

What about tools like Canva that have AI features you didn't intentionally use? If a tool auto-generated text suggestions or design elements that appeared in your final product, disclose it — even if you didn't seek it out. If you saw a suggestion and dismissed it, you don't need to disclose it.
What about Grammarly? The basic spell and grammar check features are fine on personal devices. Grammarly's "rewrite sentence," "improve tone," and similar generative features cross into AI-generated content and require disclosure if used. Note that the public version of Grammarly is banned on state-owned devices entirely — check the State of Nevada's current banned technology list if you're unsure.

Consequences

Misuse of AI in this course is treated as academic misconduct under the NS Student Code of Conduct and the LASB Academic Conduct Policy.

Consequences scale with severity and frequency:

SituationLikely outcome
First violation, minor (e.g., undisclosed permitted use)Warning and required resubmission
First violation, significant (e.g., AI-written project description)Zero on the assignment; academic misconduct report filed
Repeated violations or severe misconductF in the course, academic probation, suspension, or expulsion
The most important thing to know: "I didn't know" is not a defense. A misunderstanding about the AI policy does not excuse submitting work that violates the expectations of the course. If you're ever uncertain about a specific use, email Dr. M. before you do it — not after. That's always the right call.
