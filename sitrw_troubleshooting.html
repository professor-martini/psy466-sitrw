Topic Selection Due Sunday, May 31

You have a lot of freedom here, which is great — and occasionally paralyzing. The questions below cover the most common sticking points.

A good topic fits inside a short social media campaign and has enough empirical research behind it to support five peer-reviewed articles. The most common problem is picking something too broad — something that could be its own semester course.

A useful gut check: can you imagine a real 10th grader stopping their scroll for this? If the topic sounds like a chapter heading, it needs to be narrowed.

Topic narrowing — examples

Consent (too broad — a whole literature)

Why "going along with it" doesn't count as consent, and what to say instead


STIs (too broad — dozens of infections, each its own topic)

Why so many people with chlamydia don't know they have it — and why testing matters


Birth control (too broad — covers method comparison, effectiveness, access, etc.)

Things people do that make birth control pills less effective

Interest is a great starting point — but before you commit, do a quick literature search first. Search Google Scholar or PsycINFO for your topic and see whether you can find at least five peer-reviewed empirical articles (articles with a Methods section and a Results section reporting original data — not opinion pieces, reviews, or magazine articles).

If you can't find five articles in 10 minutes of searching, that's a signal the topic may be too niche, too recent, or more cultural than scientific. It's much easier to pivot now than after you've already invested time.

Quick check before submitting: Open Google Scholar. Search your topic + "empirical study." If you get a solid list of peer-reviewed results, you're in good shape. If you mostly get news coverage, opinion pieces, or nothing, narrow or pivot.

This is worth thinking through early. Some topics are academically interesting but hard to translate for a 16-year-old audience in a non-shaming, engaging way. A useful test: can you identify a specific misconception that a 10th or 11th grader is likely to hold about this topic? If yes, you have something to work with. If the topic is too clinical or abstract to connect to a real teen's life, consider narrowing or reframing it.

For example, "treatment of vaginismus" is a legitimate clinical topic but may be harder to frame engagingly for a teen audience than "why some people find sex painful and what can help." Same underlying science — very different entry point.

Article Submission and Annotations Due Sunday, June 7

You need five empirical articles total; you'll submit annotated summaries for three of them using the provided table. Here's where students most often go sideways.

An empirical article reports original data collected by the authors of that study. It will have a Methods section (describing how data was collected) and a Results section (reporting what was found). If it doesn't have both of those sections, it's not empirical for this assignment.

What counts — and what doesn't

A review article that summarizes other studies (no original data)

A meta-analysis (combines existing studies — no original data collection)

A Psychology Today article, magazine piece, or blog post

A book chapter

A position statement or opinion piece from a professional organization


A study that recruited participants, measured something, and reported results

A survey study with a sample, procedure, and statistical results

An experiment with conditions, participants, and findings

Unsure? Open the article and scan for a section labeled "Method" or "Methods." If you find it, you're on the right track. Then look for "Results." If both are there and the researchers collected their own data, it's empirical.

Start with PsycINFO (available through the NSU library) or Google Scholar. Both will surface peer-reviewed research. PsycINFO in particular lets you filter by "Peer Reviewed" and "Empirical Study" which saves a lot of time.

In Google Scholar, try adding terms like "study," "survey," or "experiment" to your search along with your topic. This helps surface empirical work rather than review articles.

A note on full-text access: If an article is paywalled, check whether NSU's library has access. You can also email the first author directly — researchers are often happy to share their own work. Don't use a source you haven't read in full.

The summary table walks you through the article section by section — introduction, hypothesis, method, results, explanation — and then asks you to combine those pieces into a coherent paragraph. That final paragraph is your summary.

An annotation goes one step further: it evaluates the source and connects it to your project. A strong annotation doesn't just describe what the article found — it explains why this particular study is useful for your project, what specific finding you plan to use, and how it connects to the misconception you're trying to address.

Annotation example (abbreviated)

Topic: Why people with chlamydia often don't know they have it

Weak (summary only):

"This study surveyed 400 college students about STI testing behavior. They found that 62% had never been tested. The authors concluded that testing rates are low."

Stronger (annotation):

"This study surveyed 400 college students and found that 62% had never been tested for STIs despite being sexually active, with the most common reason being 'I don't have any symptoms.' This directly supports my project's core claim — that many young people assume symptom-free means infection-free — and I will use the 62% statistic as the hook for my first post."

Section in the table What to do
APA reference Full citation with DOI. Author(s), year, title, journal, volume, pages.
Introduction 1–2 sentences: what problem is this study addressing and why does it matter?
Hypothesis State the hypothesis in your own words. Identify the predictor (independent) variable and the outcome (dependent) variable.
Method 1–2 sentences: who were the participants, what did they do, and how was the outcome measured?
Results & discussion Was each hypothesis supported, partially supported, or not supported? Explain briefly.
Explanation 1–2 sentences: what do the authors say explains these results?
Put it all together Combine steps 2–6 into one readable paragraph. Edit so it flows — don't just paste.

You need five articles, and each one should contribute something distinct to your project — not just add numbers to a list. If three of your articles are all making the same point with slightly different samples, that's a sign to diversify your sources. Ideally, different articles cover different aspects of your topic: maybe one establishes the scope of the problem, one explains a mechanism, one looks at a specific population, and one points toward a solution or intervention.

Project Description Due Sunday, June 14

This is a two-page, single-spaced document with three required components. Students who miss the mark here have usually written a literature review instead of a project description — or skipped one of the three components.

The project description has three required components. All three need to be present — missing any one of them will affect your grade significantly.

  • A summary of the research from your five empirical articles (with in-text citations and a reference list)
  • The medium you chose — TikTok/Reels video series or Instagram carousel — and a real explanation of why that format works for your specific topic and audience
  • How you plan to identify the misconceptions your audience holds and correct them using the science from your articles
The most common mistake: Students write a strong research summary, tack on "I chose TikTok because it's popular," and don't address the misconception piece at all. All three sections need genuine thought.

A misconception is a specific, nameable incorrect belief that real members of your audience are likely to hold — and that your research directly addresses. "Students don't know enough about this topic" is not a misconception. It's too vague to build a campaign around.

Misconception examples

"Students don't understand consent." (too vague)

"Many adolescents believe that silence or going along means yes."


"Students need to learn about STI prevention." (a goal, not a misconception)

"Many teens believe that if they don't have symptoms, they can't have an STI."


"Young people are misinformed about birth control." (not specific enough)

"Many teens believe that missing one pill occasionally doesn't really affect how well birth control works."

Once you've named the misconception, your project description should explain which specific article(s) provide the evidence to correct it.

Think about what your specific content needs in order to land effectively. A few questions that can help you build a real justification:

  • Does your topic work better as a visual (infographic carousel) or as a narrated explanation (video)?
  • Does your content have a natural sequence or series of steps — which might favor a multi-part video — or does it involve a comparison or set of facts that are easier to read than hear?
  • What format allows you to most clearly correct the specific misconception you identified?
  • What format would feel least "lecture-y" to a 16-year-old encountering this content while scrolling?

You don't need to cite research on social media use. You just need to make a real argument that connects your content to your format choice.

Final Social Media Campaign Submission Due by 5pm Friday, June 27

The final submission is more than just the posts. Make sure you know exactly what you need to turn in — and what "evidence-based" actually requires in practice.

The submission has two parts: the live post(s) and the written documentation. Many students post their content and consider it done — but the written documentation is a major component of what gets graded.

If you chose the TikTok/Reels video format:

  • Three posted TikTok/Reels-style videos (minimum 90 seconds total)
  • The caption/descriptive text for each video
  • A written script or transcript for each video
  • A source note for each video identifying which empirical article supports the major claims, with page numbers
  • The link to your mock account

If you chose the Instagram carousel format:

  • One Instagram carousel post (minimum five slides)
  • The caption/descriptive text for the post
  • The text that appears on each slide
  • A source note for each slide identifying which empirical article supports the major claims, with page numbers
  • The link to your mock account
No link = no grade. Without an active, accessible link to your social media account, the project cannot be graded. Test your link before submitting.

Yes — but you have flexibility in how you do it. You don't need a full APA citation on every slide or screen (that would make the post unreadable). You have two options:

  • On-screen/on-slide: A brief in-text citation where the claim appears, e.g., "(Smith & Garcia, 2022)"
  • In the caption: A note that explains which sources the post is based on, e.g., "Information in this post is drawn from research by Smith & Garcia (2022) and Nguyen et al. (2021)."

Either approach is fine. What matters is that a viewer — and Dr. M. — can tell where your claims are coming from. "Studies show..." with no traceable source doesn't meet the requirement.

This is one of the hardest parts of science communication — and it's exactly what the project is designed to practice. A few things that tend to help:

  • Lead with the misconception, not the science. "Did you know most people with chlamydia have zero symptoms?" lands differently than "Chlamydia is a common STI that is often asymptomatic."
  • Use the second person. "You might assume..." or "Here's what that means for you" keeps the audience in the frame.
  • Concrete numbers beat vague claims. "62% of sexually active students had never been tested" is more compelling than "many people don't get tested."
  • End with something actionable. Your audience needs to know what to do with this information — not just that the problem exists.

The goal is still to teach accurate science. The hook just determines whether anyone sticks around long enough to receive it.
